Daily Technical Snippet

What is hybrid search?

Hybrid search runs two retrievers at once and fuses their scores: a classic keyword engine (BM25) that rewards exact term matches, and a dense vector engine that captures meaning. Keyword search nails names, IDs, and rare jargon that embeddings blur; vectors catch paraphrases that keyword search misses entirely. For a URL-first memory product, fusing both is what stops "the exact page I saved" from vanishing just because I phrased the query differently.

Learn more

Pure vector search has a well-known failure mode: ask for an exact product code, error string, or surname and cosine similarity happily returns "semantically nearby" junk while the exact match sits at rank 30. BM25 — a decades-old sparse, term-frequency method — gets those right but is blind to synonyms and intent. Hybrid search queries both indexes and merges the ranked lists, most commonly with Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF), which combines by rank position rather than raw scores, so you don't have to normalise two incompatible scales. The usual pipeline is: retrieve wide with hybrid → fuse with RRF → optionally rerank the top slice with a cross-encoder → feed the model. For Kepler, hybrid is the cheap, high-leverage upgrade: it directly improves recall on the "find the specific URL I meant" queries that a memory product lives or dies on.
