Daily Technical Snippet

What is contextual retrieval?

Contextual retrieval fixes RAG's weakest link. A chunk ripped out of a document loses the context that made it findable, so before embedding each chunk you prepend a short LLM-written blurb that situates it ("From Alinta's FY26 report; this covers WA gas contracts…"). Anthropic reported it cuts failed retrievals by up to ~49% when paired with reranking.

Learn more

Standard RAG splits documents into chunks, embeds each, and retrieves by similarity — but a chunk like "the company secured 46 PJ" is ambiguous once separated from "Alinta" and "gas". Contextual retrieval asks a cheap model to write a 1–2 sentence context for each chunk against the whole document, prepends it, then indexes both the contextual text (dense embeddings) and a contextual BM25 (sparse) — hybrid search on better inputs. Anthropic measured retrieval-failure drops of ~35% (contextual embeddings) rising to ~49% once you rerank the top slice. For Kepler it's the natural upgrade on top of hybrid search: cheap at index time, and it directly rescues the "I saved that exact page but the chunk lost its anchor" failure a URL-first memory can't afford.
